Calorie Deficit for Men: Lose Fat Without Losing Muscle

📅 May 2026 ⏱ 7 min read 🔬 Science-based
⚡ Quick Answer

Most men should eat 300–500 calories below their TDEE to lose fat while preserving muscle. For a typical sedentary man this means roughly 1,800–2,100 kcal/day; for an active man, 2,200–2,600 kcal/day. The right number depends on your weight, height, age and activity — use our free calculator to get your exact target.

Men have naturally higher TDEE than women of the same weight due to greater muscle mass and testosterone-driven metabolic activity. This means men can typically run larger absolute deficits — but also that the temptation to cut too aggressively is greater, and the muscle loss risk when doing so is significant.

How to Calculate Your Calorie Deficit

Step 1 — Calculate Your BMR

Men BMR = (10 × weight kg) + (6.25 × height cm) − (5 × age) + 5

Example: A 35-year-old man, 85 kg, 180 cm:
BMR = (850) + (1,125) − (175) + 5 = 1,805 kcal/day

Muscle Preservation: The Critical Variable for Men

The biggest difference between a successful cut and a failed one is muscle retention. Men who cut aggressively — especially combined with high training volume — lose a disproportionate amount of muscle alongside fat, which reduces TDEE, weakens performance and produces a "skinny fat" rather than lean physique.

💡 The protein rule that changes everything

Men eating 2.0–2.4g of protein per kg of bodyweight during a deficit lose significantly more fat and significantly less muscle than those eating 1.2g/kg — even with identical total calories. For an 85kg man, this means targeting 170–204g of protein daily.

Body Recomposition: Losing Fat and Building Muscle Simultaneously

Body recomposition — losing fat and gaining muscle at the same time — is achievable for men in specific circumstances. It's not a myth, but it requires the right conditions.

Recomposition works best for: men new to training, men returning after a long break, men with higher body fat (20%+), and men on GLP-1 medications who are losing weight rapidly and need to prioritise muscle retention.

The recomposition protocol: eat at a very small deficit (150–250 kcal/day), maximise protein (2.2–2.4g/kg), and train with progressive overload resistance training 3–4x/week. Scale readings will barely move but body composition changes significantly over 12–16 weeks.

Structured Cut and Maintain Cycles

Continuous calorie restriction for more than 16 weeks leads to metabolic adaptation — your body reduces NEAT and metabolic rate in response to prolonged deficit. The solution is structured cycling:

Calorie Deficit for Shift Workers and Night Shift Men

Working rotating shifts — nights, early mornings or irregular patterns — disrupts circadian rhythms in ways that directly affect metabolism. Research shows shift workers have higher rates of obesity, metabolic syndrome and insulin resistance compared to day workers with identical calorie intake.

The key adaptations for shift working men:

— Use a lower activity multiplier than your exercise level suggests. A man who trains 4x/week but works nights should use "lightly active" (×1.375) rather than "moderately active" (×1.55) as a starting estimate.
— Eat the majority of calories during waking hours, not at 3am. Late-night eating worsens insulin sensitivity and promotes fat storage even at the same total calorie intake.
— Anchor your protein intake — regardless of shift pattern, hitting your daily protein target consistently is the most important nutritional variable.
— Track your actual weight trend for 3 weeks before adjusting — calculated TDEE will be less accurate for shift workers than for those with regular schedules.

⚠️ The shift worker metabolism trap

Men on night shifts often underestimate their calorie intake because they eat at unusual times and lose track of total daily consumption. Using a food tracking app for 2–3 weeks is especially valuable for shift workers to establish a reliable baseline.

Does a Calorie Deficit Affect Testosterone?

This is one of the most common concerns men have about cutting. The evidence is nuanced:

Moderate deficits (300–500 kcal/day) do not significantly affect testosterone in most men. Short-term studies show no meaningful hormonal suppression at this level.

Aggressive deficits (750+ kcal/day) combined with high training volume can reduce testosterone by 20–40% in some studies. The mechanism involves reduced dietary fat intake (testosterone is synthesised from cholesterol) and elevated cortisol from training stress.

Practical protection: maintain fat intake at a minimum of 20–25% of total calories, avoid deficits over 600 kcal/day, and include rest days to manage training load.

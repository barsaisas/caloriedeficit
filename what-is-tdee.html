What is TDEE? How to Calculate Your Total Daily Energy Expenditure

📅 May 2026 ⏱ 6 min read 🔬 Science-based
⚡ Quick Answer

TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure) is the total number of calories your body burns every day — including at rest and during all activity. It is your personal maintenance calorie level: eat below it to lose fat, above it to build muscle. Calculate your TDEE free in 30 seconds →

TDEE is the single most important number in nutrition. Without knowing it, any calorie target you follow — whether from a diet book, an app or a generic online guide — is essentially a guess. Knowing your personal TDEE lets you engineer your body composition with precision rather than hoping for the best.

What Makes Up Your TDEE?

Your TDEE is not one fixed number — it's the sum of four distinct components, each of which can be influenced by your lifestyle choices:

💡 The NEAT insight most people miss

NEAT (non-exercise movement) varies by up to 2,000 kcal/day between individuals with similar body sizes. This is why two people can eat the same food and have completely different weight outcomes — one fidgets, stands and walks constantly; the other sits still. NEAT is the hidden variable that makes generic calorie advice unreliable.

How to Calculate Your TDEE

The most accurate method to estimate TDEE without laboratory equipment is the Mifflin-St Jeor equation, validated in a landmark 1990 study and consistently outperforming older formulas like Harris-Benedict in head-to-head comparisons.

Step 1 — Calculate Your BMR

Men: BMR = (10 × weight kg) + (6.25 × height cm) − (5 × age) + 5
Women: BMR = (10 × weight kg) + (6.25 × height cm) − (5 × age) − 161

Step 2 — Multiply by Your Activity Factor

⚠️ The most common TDEE mistake

Most people choose "moderately active" when they should choose "lightly active" or "sedentary". If you exercise 3x/week but sit at a desk for 8+ hours, you are lightly active at best. Overestimating your activity is the number one reason calculated TDEE feels too high and weight loss stalls.

Why Your TDEE Changes Over Time

TDEE is not a fixed number for life. It changes as your body composition, activity and age change — which is why weight loss inevitably slows down if you never adjust your targets.

  • 1

    Weight loss reduces TDEE

    Less body mass = less energy required to maintain it. Losing 10 kg can reduce TDEE by 150–250 kcal/day, shrinking your deficit without you changing anything.

  • 2

    Metabolic adaptation

    During prolonged calorie restriction, your body reduces NEAT (unconscious movement) and metabolic rate as a survival mechanism. This can reduce TDEE by 10–15% beyond what body weight changes would predict.

  • 3

    Muscle gain increases TDEE

    Muscle tissue is metabolically active — each kg of muscle burns approximately 13 kcal/day at rest. Building muscle through resistance training gradually increases your TDEE over months.

  • 4

    Age reduces TDEE

    BMR decreases approximately 1–2% per decade after age 30, primarily due to gradual muscle loss. Resistance training is the most effective intervention to slow this decline.

✅ Practical rule

Recalculate your TDEE every 4–6 weeks or every 3–5 kg of weight change. This keeps your calorie target accurate as your body adapts.

TDEE for Night Shift and Shift Workers

If you work rotating shifts — nights, early mornings or irregular hours — standard TDEE calculations become less reliable. Circadian rhythm disruption reduces metabolic rate, impairs insulin sensitivity and alters hunger hormones (ghrelin and leptin), all of which affect how efficiently your body processes calories.

Research published in the Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism found that simulated night shift workers burned approximately 12–16% fewer calories during sleep compared to day workers with identical activity levels. As a practical adjustment, shift workers should consider using the activity multiplier one level below their actual activity — for example, choosing "lightly active" instead of "moderately active" — and tracking actual weight trend to validate.

Frequently Asked Questions

TDEE is the total number of calories your body burns every day — at rest plus all physical activity. Think of it as your body's daily energy budget. Eat below it and you lose weight; eat above it and you gain. It's the foundation of all nutrition planning.
BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate) is the calories your body burns at complete rest — just to keep your heart beating and organs functioning. TDEE includes your BMR plus all energy burned through movement, exercise and digestion. TDEE is always higher than BMR — typically 20–90% higher depending on your activity level.
The Mifflin-St Jeor formula estimates TDEE with approximately ±10% accuracy for most people. The biggest source of error is choosing the wrong activity multiplier — most people overestimate their activity level. Use the calculated number as a starting point and adjust after 2–3 weeks based on actual weight changes.

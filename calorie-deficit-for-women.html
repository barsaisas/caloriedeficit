Calorie Deficit for Women: How to Calculate It Right

📅 May 2026 ⏱ 7 min read 🔬 Science-based
⚡ Quick Answer

Most women should eat 300–500 calories below their TDEE to lose fat sustainably. For a sedentary woman this typically means 1,300–1,500 kcal/day; for an active woman, 1,600–1,900 kcal/day. The exact number depends on your weight, height, age, activity level and hormonal status — use our free calculator to get your personalised target.

Generic calorie advice — "eat 1,200 calories to lose weight" — ignores the biological reality that women's calorie needs vary enormously by body size, activity, age and hormonal phase. A 25-year-old athlete and a 55-year-old post-menopausal woman have completely different metabolic profiles. This guide covers both.

How to Calculate Your Calorie Deficit

Step 1 — Your BMR (the female formula)

Women BMR = (10 × weight kg) + (6.25 × height cm) − (5 × age) − 161

Example: A 32-year-old woman, 65 kg, 165 cm:
BMR = (650) + (1,031) − (160) − 161 = 1,360 kcal/day

How the Menstrual Cycle Affects Your Calorie Deficit

One of the most overlooked factors in women's nutrition is the impact of the menstrual cycle on metabolism and hunger. Understanding this prevents unnecessary panic when the scale doesn't move — or moves the wrong way — at certain times of the month.

Days 16–28

💡 What this means for your deficit

During the luteal phase (days 16–28) your body burns more calories AND hunger increases. This is normal hormonal physiology — not a sign your metabolism is broken. If you slightly exceed your calorie target during this phase, it is unlikely to affect monthly fat loss. Some women benefit from eating at maintenance during days 24–28 to reduce cravings and improve adherence for the rest of the month.

Calorie Deficit After Menopause

Menopause brings significant metabolic changes that require adjusting your calorie approach. Declining oestrogen reduces muscle mass, redistributes fat to the abdomen and lowers TDEE by 200–300 kcal/day compared to pre-menopausal levels.

⚠️ The post-menopause trap

Many women over 50 eat at the same calorie level they used successfully at 35 — and wonder why they're gaining weight. TDEE genuinely decreases with age. Recalculate using your current weight and age, not what you weighed 10 years ago.

Practical recommendations for women over 50:

— Use a gentler deficit of 200–300 kcal/day rather than 500+ to minimise muscle loss
— Increase protein to 2.0–2.2g per kg of bodyweight — higher than younger women need
— Prioritise resistance training 3x/week to counteract muscle loss
— Recalculate TDEE every 4–6 weeks as body composition changes faster at this stage

Common Mistakes Women Make with Calorie Deficits

Eating too little. The "more deficit = more results" logic fails because extreme restriction triggers muscle loss, hormonal disruption and metabolic adaptation. A deficit of 500+ kcal/day works short-term but almost always leads to rebound weight gain.

Not accounting for the luteal phase. Increased hunger in the two weeks before menstruation is biological, not a willpower failure. Planning for slightly higher intake during this phase leads to better monthly consistency than white-knuckling through it.

Using a man's calorie target. Women have lower BMR than men of the same weight due to higher body fat percentage and lower muscle mass. A generic "1,500 calories" recommendation may be appropriate for a woman or it may represent a 700-calorie deficit — without knowing your TDEE, you can't tell.

Not recalculating as weight changes. Losing 10 kg reduces your TDEE by approximately 150–200 kcal. If you keep eating the same amount, your deficit disappears and weight loss stalls — even though you haven't changed your habits.

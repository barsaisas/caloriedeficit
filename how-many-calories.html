How Many Calories Should I Eat to Lose Weight?

📅 Updated May 2026 ⏱ 7 min read 🔬 Science-based
⚡ Quick Answer

Most adults should eat 300–500 calories below their TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure) to lose 0.25–0.5 kg per week sustainably. For a typical adult woman this means roughly 1,400–1,700 kcal/day; for a man, 1,700–2,100 kcal/day. Your exact number depends on your weight, height, age and activity level — use our free calculator to get your personalised target in 30 seconds.

The internet is full of generic "eat 1,200 calories" advice that ignores the fact that your calorie needs are completely individual. A 5'4" sedentary woman and a 6'2" active man have vastly different requirements. This guide explains exactly how to calculate the right number for your body — and why getting it right makes the difference between sustainable fat loss and frustrating yo-yo dieting.

Step 1 — Calculate Your TDEE

Before you can create a deficit, you need to know your TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure) — the total number of calories your body burns in a day including all activity. This is your true maintenance level: eat below it and you lose weight, eat above it and you gain.

TDEE is calculated in two steps. First, your BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate) — the calories your body burns at complete rest just to keep you alive. Then that number is multiplied by an activity factor.

The Mifflin-St Jeor Formula (Most Accurate)

The Mifflin-St Jeor equation is the gold standard for BMR estimation, validated across large population studies and recommended by the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics:

Men: BMR = (10 × weight kg) + (6.25 × height cm) − (5 × age) + 5
Women: BMR = (10 × weight kg) + (6.25 × height cm) − (5 × age) − 161

Then multiply your BMR by your activity level:

Activity LevelDescriptionMultiplier
SedentaryDesk job, little or no exercise× 1.2
Lightly active1–3 days/week light exercise× 1.375
Moderately active3–5 days/week moderate exercise× 1.55
Very active6–7 days/week hard training× 1.725
AthletePhysical job + daily training× 1.9
📊 Example calculation

35-year-old woman, 68 kg, 165 cm, lightly active: BMR = (10×68) + (6.25×165) − (5×35) − 161 = 1,445 kcal. TDEE = 1,445 × 1.375 = 1,987 kcal/day. To lose 0.5 kg/week she should eat approximately 1,612 kcal/day.

Step 2 — Choose Your Calorie Deficit

Once you know your TDEE, you subtract a deficit to create weight loss. The size of your deficit determines how fast you lose — but bigger is not always better. An overly aggressive deficit causes muscle loss, fatigue, hormonal disruption and almost always leads to rebound weight gain.

Research consistently shows that a deficit of 300–500 kcal/day (targeting 0.5 kg/week) produces the best long-term results. It's fast enough to see clear progress within 2–3 weeks, but moderate enough to preserve muscle mass and maintain energy levels. A 2020 meta-analysis in Obesity Reviews confirmed that slower weight loss rates lead to significantly better weight maintenance at 12 months.

⚠️ Never go below your BMR

Eating below your BMR forces your body into starvation mode — it breaks down muscle for energy and slows your metabolism. For most women this means never going below 1,200 kcal/day; for men, 1,500 kcal/day.

Step 3 — Set Your Macros

Calories determine whether you lose weight. Macros determine what you lose — fat or muscle. Getting your protein right is the single most important nutritional factor during a calorie deficit.

Calories on GLP-1 Medications (Ozempic, Wegovy, Mounjaro)

💊 Special considerations for semaglutide and tirzepatide users

If you're on a GLP-1 agonist, the standard calorie advice changes significantly. These medications suppress appetite so effectively that most patients naturally eat 1,000–1,400 kcal/day — well below their usual intake. The critical risk is muscle loss, not calorie intake itself.

Research published in NEJM on the SURMOUNT trials showed that participants on tirzepatide lost an average of 20% of body weight — but up to 40% of that loss was lean mass in patients who didn't prioritise protein. This is why our calculator has a dedicated GLP-1 mode that automatically increases your protein target to 2.2 g/kg to protect muscle during accelerated fat loss.

If you're on Ozempic, Wegovy or Mounjaro, focus on:

— Hitting at least 1.6–2.2 g of protein per kg of bodyweight every day
— Eating at least 1,000–1,200 kcal/day even when you're not hungry
— Including resistance training 2–3 times per week
— Following your prescriber's specific guidance on caloric intake

Why Generic Calorie Advice Fails

The ubiquitous "eat 1,200 calories" recommendation comes from research conducted in the 1970s on small groups of women. Applied universally, it creates deficits that are far too aggressive for some people (a large active man eating 1,200 kcal is in a 1,500 kcal deficit) and potentially insufficient for others.

Your actual calorie needs are determined by your unique combination of body composition, metabolic rate, activity level, age and hormonal profile. A calorie target should be a personalised starting point — not a universal prescription — and adjusted every 2–3 weeks based on your actual weight trend.

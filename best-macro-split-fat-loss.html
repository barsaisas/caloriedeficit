Best Macro Split for Fat Loss: Protein, Carbs and Fat Explained

📅 May 2026 ⏱ 7 min read 🔬 Science-based
⚡ Quick Answer

The best macro split for fat loss is protein 30–40% · fat 25–35% · carbs 25–40%. In practice: set protein first at 1.6–2.2g per kg of bodyweight, allocate 25–30% of calories to fat, and fill the remainder with carbs. Total calories matter more than the exact ratio — but getting protein right is the single most impactful macro decision you can make. Calculate your personal macros free →

The internet is full of conflicting macro advice — low carb vs low fat, keto vs flexible dieting, carnivore vs plant-based. The science cuts through the noise: the macro split that produces the best fat loss results is the one you can sustain, as long as protein is high enough. Everything else is secondary.

What Are Macros and Why Do They Matter for Fat Loss?

Macronutrients are the three categories of food that provide energy: protein, carbohydrates and fat. Each gram provides a specific amount of calories:

MacroCalories per gramPrimary roleFat loss priority
Protein4 kcal/gMuscle preservation, satiety, tissue repair🔴 Critical
Carbohydrates4 kcal/gTraining fuel, brain function, fibre🟡 Flexible
Fat9 kcal/gHormones, vitamin absorption, satiety🟡 Flexible

Total calories determine whether you lose or gain weight. Your macro split determines what you lose — specifically the ratio of fat to muscle tissue. Two people eating identical calories with different macro splits will get different body composition results over time.

💡 The rule that simplifies everything

Don't start with percentages. Start with protein: multiply your bodyweight in kg by 1.8–2.0. That's your daily protein target in grams. Convert to calories (× 4), subtract from your total calorie budget, allocate 25–30% to fat, and fill the rest with carbs. Our calculator does all of this automatically.

How to Calculate Your Personal Macros — Step by Step

1

Calculate your TDEE and calorie target

You need your daily calorie target before you can set macros. Use our calculator to get your TDEE and subtract your chosen deficit (300–500 kcal for moderate fat loss).

2

Set protein first

Multiply your bodyweight in kg by 1.8–2.2 to get your daily protein target in grams.

Example: 75kg × 2.0 = 150g protein · 150 × 4 = 600 kcal from protein
3

Allocate fat

Take 25–30% of your total calorie target for fat. Never go below 20%.

Example: 1,600 kcal × 0.27 = 432 kcal from fat · 432 ÷ 9 = 48g fat
4

Fill the remainder with carbs

Subtract protein and fat calories from total target. The rest goes to carbs.

Example: 1,600 - 600 (protein) - 432 (fat) = 568 kcal · 568 ÷ 4 = 142g carbs
5

Final check — verify totals

Add up all macro calories and confirm they match your daily target (±50 kcal is fine).

150g P (600) + 48g F (432) + 142g C (568) = 1,600 kcal ✓

A 2020 meta-analysis in The BMJ comparing low-carb and low-fat diets found virtually identical fat loss at 6 and 12 months when total calories were matched. The single most important macro variable across all diet types is protein — not carb or fat distribution.

⚠️ The low protein trap

The most damaging macro mistake is eating too little protein. Many popular diet plans (low fat diets from the 1990s, extreme low calorie diets, some plant-based approaches) provide only 10–15% of calories from protein. In a calorie deficit, this leads to significant muscle loss — reducing TDEE permanently and producing poor body composition despite weight loss on the scale.

Common Macro Mistakes That Slow Fat Loss

Obsessing over the ratio instead of the total. If you're hitting 40% protein but eating 500 kcal over your target, no macro split will produce fat loss. Calories first, macros second.

Cutting fat too low. Fat is essential for testosterone, oestrogen, cortisol and other hormones. Dropping below 20% of calories from fat can cause hormonal disruption, joint pain and poor micronutrient absorption. The minimum is 0.7g per kg of bodyweight.

Eliminating carbs without a reason. Carbohydrates are not inherently fattening. They only contribute to fat gain when consumed in excess of total calorie needs. For active people, low-carb approaches can impair training performance and recovery without providing additional fat loss benefits.

Not adjusting macros as weight changes. As you lose weight, your protein target (set in grams per kg) decreases proportionally. Recalculate every 4–6 weeks or every 5 kg lost.
